Kuiper Belt

The icy frontier beyond Neptune.

30 - 50 AU from Sun Mass: 25x Asteroid Belt Objects: > 100,000 Notable: Pluto Type: Trans-Neptunian

Overview

The Kuiper belt is a circumstellar disc in the outer Solar System, extending from the orbit of Neptune at 30 AU to approximately 50 AU from the Sun. It is similar to the asteroid belt, but is far larger—20 times as wide and 20 to 200 times as massive.

Like the asteroid belt, it consists mainly of small bodies or remnants from when the Solar System formed. While many asteroids are composed primarily of rock and metal, most Kuiper belt objects are composed largely of frozen volatiles (termed "ices"), such as methane, ammonia, and water.

Key Facts

Physical

  • Total Mass: ~0.1 Earth Mass
  • Width: ~20 AU
  • Composition: Ice, Rock, Methane
  • Formation: 4.6 Billion Years

Orbital

  • Inner Edge: 30 AU (Neptune)
  • Outer Edge: ~50 AU
  • Orbital Period: 165-310 Years

Types

  • Classical: Cubewanos (Main Belt)
  • Resonant: Plutinos (2:3 Neptune)
  • Scattered: Extended Orbits

Dwarf Planets

  • Pluto: 2,377 km
  • Haumea: 1,632 km
  • Makemake: 1,430 km

History & Exploration

The Kuiper Belt was theorized by astronomer Gerard Kuiper in 1951, though similar ideas had been proposed earlier. The first Kuiper Belt Object (after Pluto) was discovered in 1992 by David Jewitt and Jane Luu.

NASA's New Horizons spacecraft made history by flying past Pluto in 2015, providing the first close-up images of this dwarf planet. In 2019, it flew past Arrokoth (2014 MU69), the most distant object ever visited by a spacecraft, giving us insights into pristine Kuiper Belt objects.
