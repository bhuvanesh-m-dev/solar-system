The Sun

The Star at the Center of our Solar System.

Star Type: G2V Radius: 696,340 km Mass: 1.989 x 10^30 kg 8 Planets Age: 4.6 Billion Years

Overview

The Sun is the star at the center of the Solar System. It is a nearly perfect ball of hot plasma, heated to incandescence by nuclear fusion reactions in its core. The Sun radiates this energy mainly as light, ultraviolet, and infrared radiation, and is the most important source of energy for life on Earth.

Its diameter is about 1.39 million kilometers (864,000 miles), or 109 times that of Earth. Its mass is about 330,000 times that of Earth, accounting for about 99.86% of the total mass of the Solar System. Roughly three-quarters of the Sun's mass consists of hydrogen; the rest is mostly helium.

Key Facts

Physical

  • Radius: 696,340 km (109 x Earth)
  • Mass: 1.989 x 10^30 kg
  • Surface Area: 6.09 x 10^12 km²
  • Gravity: 274 m/s² (28 x Earth)
  • Composition: Hydrogen, Helium

Characteristics

  • Spectral Type: G2V (Yellow Dwarf)
  • Surface Temp: ~5,500 °C (5,778 K)
  • Core Temp: ~15 Million °C
  • Luminosity: 3.828 x 10^26 W

Rotation

  • At Equator: ~25 Days
  • At Poles: ~35 Days
  • Axial Tilt: 7.25°

Atmosphere

  • Photosphere: Visible Surface
  • Chromosphere: Above Photosphere
  • Corona: Outer Atmosphere

Internal Structure

The Sun's structure consists of several layers. The Core is the innermost layer where nuclear fusion occurs, converting hydrogen into helium and releasing vast amounts of energy. Surrounding the core is the Radiative Zone, where energy travels outward through radiation.

Above that is the Convective Zone, where hot plasma rises, cools, and sinks, creating convection currents. The visible surface is the Photosphere. Above it lie the Chromosphere and the Corona, which extends millions of kilometers into space.

The Planetary System

The Sun is orbited by eight major planets, numerous dwarf planets, asteroids, comets, and other bodies. The four inner planets are terrestrial (rocky), while the four outer planets are giant planets.

Inner Planets

  • Mercury: Closest, smallest
  • Venus: Hottest, thick atmosphere
  • Earth: Supports life, liquid water
  • Mars: Red planet, potential for life

History & Exploration

Humans have observed the Sun since antiquity, often worshipping it as a deity. Scientific understanding grew with the development of telescopes and spectroscopy.

Modern exploration involves dedicated solar observatories. The SOHO (Solar and Heliospheric Observatory) has been watching the Sun since 1995. The Parker Solar Probe, launched in 2018, is currently flying closer to the Sun than any previous spacecraft, studying its corona and solar wind.
