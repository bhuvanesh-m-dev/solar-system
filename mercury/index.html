Mercury

The Swift Planet, closest to the Sun.

0.39 AU from Sun Radius: 2,439 km Mass: 3.30 x 10^23 kg 0 Moons Discovered: Antiquity

Overview

Mercury is the smallest planet in the Solar System and the closest to the Sun. Its orbit around the Sun takes 87.97 Earth days, the shortest of all the Sun's planets. It is named after the Roman god Mercurius (Mercury), god of commerce, messenger of the gods, and mediator between gods and mortals, corresponding to the Greek god Hermes.

Like Venus, Mercury orbits the Sun within Earth's orbit as an inferior planet, and its apparent distance from the Sun as viewed from Earth never exceeds 28°. This proximity to the Sun means the planet can only be seen near the western or eastern horizon during the early evening or early morning.

Internal Structure & Geology

Mercury appears to have a solid silicate crust and mantle overlying a solid, iron sulfide outer core layer, a deeper liquid core layer, and a solid inner core. The planet's density is the second highest in the Solar System at 5.427 g/cm3, only slightly less than Earth's density.

Mercury's core occupies a large fraction of its volume, about 55% (compared to 17% for Earth). Research suggests that Mercury's iron core is larger than expected because the outer layers were stripped away by a giant impact early in the Solar System's history.

Mercury Moons

Mercury does not have any moons. It is one of only two planets in our solar system (the other being Venus) that does not have a natural satellite.

History & Exploration

Mercury has been visited by two spacecraft. Mariner 10 flew by in 1974 and 1975, mapping 45% of the surface. The MESSENGER orbiter launched in 2004, orbited Mercury over 4,000 times in four years before exhausting its fuel and crashing into the planet's surface on April 30, 2015.

The BepiColombo spacecraft, a joint mission between the European Space Agency (ESA) and the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA), was launched in 2018 and is scheduled to arrive at Mercury in 2025.
